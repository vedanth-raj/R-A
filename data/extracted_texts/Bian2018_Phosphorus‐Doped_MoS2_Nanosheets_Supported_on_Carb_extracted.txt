Phosphorus doped MoS2 nanosheets supported on carbon cloths 
as efficient hydrogen generation electrocatalysts 
Luozhen Bian,[a,b],#  Wei Gao,[c,d],# Jiamin Sun,[a,b] Mingming Han,[a,b] Fulin Li,[a,b] Zhaofeng Gao,[a,b] Lei 
Shu,[d,e] Ning Han,[f] Zai-xing Yang,*[a,b] Aimin Song,[b] Yongquan Qu,*[c] Johnny C. Ho*[d,e] 
Abstract: 
Highly 
efficient 
and 
stable 
non-noble-metal 
electrocatalysts for hydrogen generation are desired for sustainable 
development of energy conversion and storage. Here, a facile low-
temperature phosphidation process is developed for phosphorus 
doping into molybdenum disulfide (MoS2) nanosheets directly 
synthesized on carbon cloths to function as an integrated electrode 
for efficient hydrogen evolution reaction. As compared with the un-
doped MoS2 nanosheets, the optimal phosphorus-doped (3.3 at%) 
MoS2 nanosheets exhibit the substantially lower overpotentials of 
133 mV and 189 mV to drive the current densities of 20 mA cm-2 and 
100 mA cm-2, respectively, with an impressive small Tafel slope of 
67.0 mV dec-1. The excellent stability in acid media also guarantees 
the phosphorus-doped MoS2 nanosheet catalysts as promising 
candidates towards hydrogen generation for practical applications. 
Introduction 
In the past decades, hydrogen is not only considered as one of 
the most promising future clean fuel, but also regarded as an 
ideal carrier to store electricity generated from other renewable 
energy sources in large scale for practical applications.[1-3] 
Particularly, water electrolysis is a simple and eco-friendly 
approach to simultaneously generate hydrogen and oxygen in 
acidic, alkaline or neutral media.[4] The efficiency of these 
hydrogen and oxygen evolutions is closely related to the 
selection of electrode materials, and hence, it is important to 
develop low-cost, highly-active and stable electrocatalysts for 
sustainable water splitting. At the same time, although Pt is 
known to be a very electrocatalytic active and stable material for 
hydrogen evolution reaction (HER), its high cost and scarcity 
greatly restrict its industrial utilizations.[5-6] In this regard, 
extensive efforts have been devoted to investigate highly 
efficient, non-noble metal electrocatalyst materials with low 
overpotentials and large HER current densities. Among many 
promising recent candidate materials, due to the large capacities 
of well-known catalytically active edge sites and excellent 
stability, two-dimensional (2D) transition metal dichalcogenides 
(TMDs), such as molybdenum disulfide (MoS2) nanosheets, 
have attracted substantial attentions for making electrochemical 
hydrogen production efficient.[7-10]  
Previously, MoS2 was researched as a potential candidate for 
HER because of its low free energy of hydrogen adsorption, as 
demonstrated by theoretical and experimental analyses.[11] 
Nevertheless, the bulk MoS2 crystal is usually performed as the 
poor HER electrocatalyst due to its relatively low content of 
active catalytic sites and small specific surface area. In this case, 
many methods have been employed to enhance its quantities of 
electrochemical active sites and surface area since then. For 
example, defect engineering is a newly developed method to re-
construct the surface of MoS2 with more edges and defects, 
which helps to expose more active catalytic sites of unsaturated 
sulfur atoms.[12-14] On the other hand, controllable disorder 
engineering has also been demonstrated in oxygen-incorporated 
MoS2 ultrathin nanosheets for the efficient hydrogen evolution.[13] 
Furthermore, 
interface 
engineering 
on 
the 
surface 
of 
nanostructured MoS2 by building a heterogeneous catalyst is 
also well established to increase the number of surface active 
sites and correspondingly to improve the catalytic properties.[15-
17] Recently, doping engineering has as well been proved as a 
very effective technique to create active catalytic sites on MoS2 
nanosheets. Because of the earth abundance and good 
stability,[18-20] several dopants, such as phosphorus (P), carbon 
(C) and nitrogen (N), etc., are widely employed to dope into 
MoS2 nanosheets in order to lower the energy barrier of the 
water-dissociation step and to facilitate the desorption of the as-
formed hydroxide species to accelerate HER processes.[12-13, 21] 
Specifically, Liu et al. have reported that P dopants in MoS2 
matrix can significantly enhance the HER activity by not only 
increasing the quantity of intrinsic basal plane active sites, but 
also enhancing the conductivity of the materials.[22] Moreover, it 
would be extremely attractive to prepare an integrated electrode 
of phosphorus-doped MoS2 on a flexible substrate to afford the 
large current density for practical utilizations.

doped MoS2 nanosheets on carbon cloths (CC) directly as an

Results and Discussion 
Here, MoS2 nanosheets directly supported on carbon cloths 
were prepared via a simple hydrothermal method, and their 
morphologies were studied by SEM. As shown in Figure 1a, the 
sheet-like morphology of MoS2 is revealed with the size of 
hundreds of nanometers, while the inset image exhibits the 
uniform distribution of nanosheets on carbon cloths. Doping 
phosphorous element is then realized by using a low-
temperature 
gas-phase 
phosphidation 
method, 
where 
NaH2PO2ꞏH2O is functioned as the phosphorous evaporation 
source with various time duration intervals (namely, 30, 60 and 
90 minutes). Previously, Tour et al. reported the formation of 
mixture phase of MoS2(1-x)Px with enhanced catalytic properties 
for HER by thermally annealing mixtures of MoS2 and red 
phosphorus at up to 1023 K but the nanosheet dimension and 
morphology are inevitably altered.[23] In contrast to the work 
mentioned 
above, 
in 
this 
work, 
an 
inorganic 
salt 
of 
NaH2PO2ꞏH2O is employed as the phosphorous source to dope 
MoS2 nanosheets at a much lower temperature of 573 K, 
resulting in the preservation of the nanosheet morphology. As 
displayed in Figure 1b and Supporting Information Figure S1a to 
S1d, the morphology of all P-doped MoS2 nanosheets supported 
on CC and processed with different doping duration (i.e. 30, 60 
and 90 minutes) is well preserved as compared to the as-
prepared un-doped MoS2 nanosheets (Figure 1a). In addition, 
the EDS spectra further confirm the existence of P dopants in 
MoS2 nanosheets (Figure 1c). It is obvious that the P-doped 
MoS2 nanosheets show a higher P doping content with a longer 
P doping duration. In specific, the doping concentration is 
determined to be 2.1 at%, 3.3 at% and 4.9 at% for the samples 
processed for 30 min, 60 min and 90 min of P doping, 
respectively. At the same time, small peaks of oxygen element 
with energy of ~ 0.55Kev are observed in EDS spectra of all 
samples. The oxygen element may come from the adsorbed 
water molecules and surface hydroxide species when exposing 
to air, even after calcining in Ar. And for P-doped MoS2, partial 
oxidation of phosphorus can also lead to the existence of 
oxygen element. The crystal structure and phase of the as-
prepared samples are then studied by X-ray diffractometer 
(XRD). The corresponding XRD pattern exhibit the hexagonal 
structure of MoS2, which perfectly agrees the crystal phase of 
1T-molybdenite of MoS2 (Supporting Information Figure 1e).[24] 
Also, broad peaks at about 26 and 43 degrees can be ascribed 
to the carbon cloth as reported in the literature.[25-26] More 
importantly, the thorough XRD characterization indicates that all 
the P-doped MoS2 samples have the crystalline structure of 2H-
MoS2 (PDF# 37-1492),[23-24] which is uniquely different from the 
un-doped MoS2/CC sample. This conversion of crystalline 
structure from 1T phase to 2H phase is mainly related to the 
poor thermal stability of 1T-MoS2 as well as the rapid 
transformation to thermodynamically stable 2H phase occurred 
during the low- temperature phosphidation process.[24] All these 
findings demonstrate that our low-temperature P doping in MoS2 
nanosheets can preserve their morphology and lead to the 
effective phase transformation from 1T to 2H crystalline 
structures.

the P atoms are successfully and homogeneously doped into 
MoS2 without deteriorating the nanosheet morphology. 
Apart from the morphology, crystallinity and composition, it is 
also important to assess the surface chemical states of all MoS2 
samples. In this case, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) 
is utilized to characterize any change in the surface chemical 
state before and after P doping in the MoS2 nanosheets. As 
depicted in the P 2p spectra in Figure 3a, there is not any peak 
observed for the un-doped MoS2 sample, while obvious peaks 
are found to be located at 133.4 eV for all P-doped MoS2 
samples. This peak is believed to be due to the surface oxidized 
phosphate species when P atoms are doped into the lattice of 
MoS2.[29-30] On the other hand, for the Mo 3d and S 2p spectra as 
shown in Figure 3b and c, there are a tiny peak at 225.6 eV as 
well as strong peaks at 228.3 eV and 231.5 eV, corresponding 
to the Mo-S bond, Mo4+ species of Mo 3d5/2 and Mo 3d3/2,[17] and 
another set of strong peaks at 161.2 eV and 162.3 eV for 2p3/2 
and 2p1/2 orbitals of S2- species in un-doped MoS2, 
respectively.[13, 28] After P doping, the spectra of Mo 3d and S 2p 
of MoS2 are still similar to those of the un-doped one, except for 
the apparent shifts in their binding energies. Explicitly, the peaks 
of Mo 3d spectra are shifted to 226.0 eV, 228.9 eV and 232.0 eV 
correspondingly; while for S 2p spectra, the peaks are moved to 
161.7 eV and 162.9 eV, respectively. These peak shifts may be 
related to the low-temperature annealing process, indicating that

To evaluate their kinetic properties, Tafel equation is employed 
to describe the relationship between current and overpotential: 
η = b log (j/j0) 
where η is the overpotential (mV), b is the Tafel slope (mV/dec), 
j is the current density (mA cm-2) and j0 is the exchange current 
density (mA cm-2).[34] As extracted from Figure 4a, after iR 
correction, the Tafel slope of 3.3 at% P-doped MoS2 (60 min 
doping duration) is found to be 67.0 mV/dec, which is smaller 
than the ones of 2.1 at% P-doped nanosheet (77.6 mV/dec), 4.9 
at% P-doped nanosheets (71.1 mV/dec), undoped nanosheet 
(102.9 mV/dec) and annealed control sample (138.9 mV/dec). 
Based on the Tafel slope without iR correction (shown in 
Supporting Information Figure S2), MoS2 nanosheets treated 
with an optimal P doping of 60 min also show the superior 
electrochemical HER activity. It is obvious that an optimal P 
doping (i.e. 3.3 at%) is needed to enhance the number of active 
catalytic sites and to improve the conductivity in order to achieve 
the superior electrochemical HER activity of MoS2 nanosheets 
directly synthesized on carbon cloths as an integrated electrode 
system. As shown in Supporting Information Table S1, by using 
the adopted simple low temperature annealing process, HER 
performance of the as-prepared P-MoS2 nanosheets on CC is 
similar to the other researches.[13, 17, 35-37] To further study the 
difference in their intrinsic properties, the electrochemical active 
surface area (ECSA) established from the electrochemical 
double-layer capacitance of the catalyst surface is as well 
utilized to compare the difference in catalytic active sites.[38] 
Because ECSA is proportional to the electrochemical double-
layer capacitance (Cdl) in the non-Faradaic potential region, 
herein, cyclic voltammogram (CV) curves with various scan rates 
are measured to calculate the Cdl values. CV curves of un-doped 
MoS2 and P-doped (3.3 at%) MoS2 nanosheets are then 
scanned with the rate of 10, 20, 30, 40 and 50 mV/s and 
complied in Supporting Information Figure S3. The extracted Cdl 
values of un-doped MoS2 and P-doped (3.3 at%) MoS2 samples 
are found to be 7.0 and 17.6 mF cm-2, respectively (Figure 4c), 
illustrating that the P doping can indeed result in more active 
sites for the more efficient catalytic hydrogen generation. 
Furthermore, the catalytic activity normalized to ECSA is also 
given in Supporting Information Figure S4, suggesting the better 
intrinsic activity of P-doped (3.3 at%) MoS2 nanosheets, as 
compared with it of un-doped MoS2.

The stable overpotentials (135 mV, without iR corrections) with 
insignificant fluctuations are observed to maintain the current 
density of 20 mA cm-2 in acidic solutions over 10 hours for the P-
doped (3.3 at%) MoS2 nanosheets as illustrated in the set of

nanosheets preserve the excellent performances in activity with 
negligible loss of the current density, which further certifies their 
superior catalytic durability in acidic media. In general, the HER 
activity of MoS2 nanosheets based on their exposed edges and 
catalytic sites would easily reach the limitation owing to the poor 
inter-particle (or inter-domain) electron transport for the 
insufficient conductivity.[9, 12] Therefore, chemical doping is 
widely considered as an effective approach to improve the HER 
activity of their inert basal plane sites in MoS2 as well as to 
increase the intrinsic electrical conductivity.[12, 21-22] In this work, 
as demonstrated by XPS (Figure 3), the S constituents has been 
partially replaced by P atoms in the MoS2 crystal. Evidently, this 
substitution would induce more active unsaturated sites and 
defects in MoS2 microstructures, resulting in the better catalytic 
property for hydrogen generation. The increased activity may be 
related to the modified electronic structure of P-doped MoS2. A 
smaller band gap is observed as compared with pristine MoS2 
after doping P, resulting in the lower adsorption free energy of 
hydrogen, as previous literatures demonstrated.[22, 23]  However, 
with the increase of P content, the electrocatalytic performance 
of MoS2 nanosheets (e.g. 4.9 at% P-doped sample with 90 min 
doping duration) becomes degraded, which may be contributed 
to the conducting behavior switch of MoS2 by increasing the 
content of intercalated P in MoS2, leading in a ruined 
conductance during the process of hydrogen generation. Also, 
for the longer annealing time, the re-structure of P-doped MoS2 
may be benefited to further improve the crystallinity with less 
active site and defects. However, the detailed reason for 
electrocatalytic performance degradation of highly doped (4.9 
at%) P-MoS2 is complex and requires more thorough studies in 
the near future. Anyway, all these findings have clearly 
confirmed the favorable effect of P-doped MoS2 nanosheets 
directly synthesized on carbon cloths as an attractive integrated 
electrode with the superior catalytic properties for hydrogen 
generation.  
Conclusions 
In summary, a facile post-growth phosphorus doping into MoS2 
nanosheets directly synthesized on carbon cloths is successfully 
developed 
by 
utilizing 
low-temperature 
phosphidation. 
Phosphorus doping is found to change the surface chemical 
states of MoS2 nanosheets, increasing their surface catalytic 
sites and facilitating the better conductivity. The effect of 
different doping content and annealing durations are also 
investigated. The optimal phosphorus doped (3.3 at%) MoS2 
nanosheets exhibit the substantially improved catalytic activity 
for HER with a low overpotential of 133 mV to drive the current 
density of 20 mA cm-2 and a small Tafel slope of 67.0 mV/dec, 
as compared with the un-doped MoS2 nanosheets. All these 
demonstrate the technological potency of our phosphidation 
approach to modify MoS2 nanosheets to improve their catalytic 
properties for hydrogen generation. 
Experimental Section 
Nanosheet synthesis 
Carbon cloths were washed with 10% HCl, ethanol and distilled water 
with ultrasonication sequentially, and then dried in air. MoS2 nanosheets 
supported on carbon cloths (MoS2/CC) were then prepared using an as-
reported hydrothermal method with some modification.[39] In detail, a 
piece of carbon cloth (1 cm by 3 cm) was immersed into 15 ml of 
transparent solution, containing 0.5 mmol of Na2MoO4ꞏ2H2O and 1 mmol 
of CS(NH2)2. Next, the mixture was sealed in a Teflon-lined autoclave 
and heated at 373 K for 20 hours. After cooling to room temperature, the 
obtained MoS2 nanosheets supported on carbon cloths were washed 
with distilled water and ethanol alternatively for several times and then 
dried at 333 K overnight. By carefully measuring the mass of carbon cloth 
before and after synthesizing MoS2, the loading mass density is 
calculated to be 2.8 mg cm-2. 
To prepare P-doped MoS2 nanosheets, a low-temperature phosphidation 
method using NaH2PO2ꞏH2O as the phosphorous source was performed. 
Typically, 300 mg of NaH2PO2ꞏH2O and the obtained MoS2/CC sample 
were placed in the upstream and downstream of a quartz furnace tube, 
respectively. The sample was then annealed at 673 K for 60 minutes with 
a temperature ramping of 10 K/min under the Argon (Ar) flow of 100 
sccm. After cooling to room temperature with the continuous Ar purge, 
the P-doped MoS2/CC catalysts were obtained. In order to study the 
effect of different annealing times on the catalytic property, the as-
synthesized MoS2 was treated under the identical process conditions for 
30 and 90 minutes, respectively. 
Materials characterization 
Surface morphologies of the obtained samples were examined using 
scanning electron microscope (SEM, FEI Company, Oregon, USA/Philips 
XL30, Philips Electronics, Amsterdam, Netherlands). Crystal structures 
were determined by collecting XRD pattern on a Philips powder 
diffractometer using Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å) and imaging with a 
high-resolution TEM (HRTEM, JEOL 2100F, JEOL Co., Ltd., Tokyo, 
Japan). Elemental mappings were then performed using an energy 
dispersive x-ray (EDS) detector attached to a JEOL 2100F, and the 
chemical composition of the samples was measured. The chemical state 
of the as-prepared MoS2 and P-doped MoS2 nanosheets were examined 
by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (ULVAC-PHI5802). For the TEM 
studies, the nanosheets were first suspended in ethanol solution by 
ultrasonication and then drop-casted onto the grid for the subsequent 
characterization. 
Electrochemical measurement 
All the electrochemical tests were performed on a Gamry G300 
electrochemical workstation (Gamry Instruments, USA). A setup of three-
electrode system was employed to measure the electrochemical activity 
and stability of P-doped MoS2/CC, where the electrocatalysts, saturated 
calomel electrode (SCE) and Ti plate functioned as working electrode, 
reference electrode and counter electrode, respectively. The geometrical 
active area of P-doped MoS2/CC was defined by the silicon rubber for 
immersing into electrolytes. Linear sweeping voltammetry (LSV) curves
